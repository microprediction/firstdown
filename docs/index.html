Stop Shy of the First Down

Conventional play treats the first-down marker as a target to be reached whenever possible. Measured in expected points, that objective is not always optimal: on first down, a ball-carrier able only to reach the marker can be better off being downed a yard short. The figures below let you examine the argument directly.

Figure 1

Drive value versus where the carrier is downed

Consider a first-down play. The curve below plots the expected points for the remainder of the drive as a function of where the ball-carrier is downed, under a simple "advance the chains" continuation. It is a single, consistent measure across the whole range — and it has an interior maximum located before the line to gain.

Drag the marker along the field. The corresponding point on the curve moves with it; note that crossing the line to gain lowers, rather than raises, the drive's expected points.

Figure 2

The distribution of yards gained on first down

Across 33,951 first-and-ten rushes (2009–2013), the number gaining exactly the ten yards needed for the first down (383) falls well below both neighbours — 1,058 at nine yards and 634 at eleven. This pronounced local minimum, present in the raw integer-yard counts rather than any binning, is consistent with the marker acting as a behavioural target.

The highlighted bar sits exactly on the line to gain. Data: microprediction/nflMarkov.

Figure 3

Field position in expected points

The common unit for the analysis is the expected points of a possession as a function of field position: approximately zero near one's own twenty, rising to about four near the opponent's. As a rough guide, fifteen yards correspond to one point and a possession to roughly forty yards of field position.

Drag along the curve to read off the value at any field position.

Figure 4

Comparing 2nd-and-1 with 1st-and-10

The two states are compared from the same point on the field: taking the first down (1st-and-10), or being downed a yard short (2nd-and-1) and advancing the chains on the following downs. Simulating the continuation many times yields the mean expected points of each. The conversion probabilities and field position can be varied.

Run the simulation, then adjust the parameters and re-run.

Figure 5

Run versus pass on 3rd-and-1

A related question: on third-and-one, the run converts more often, but the pass gains substantially more when it succeeds. The decision tree below evaluates each call in expected points, accounting for a punt on a failed conversion. The preferred call depends on field position.

Summary

Under an expected-points objective, the first-down marker is an accounting threshold rather than a goal. Because a short-yardage conversion is highly likely and tends to advance the ball further, being downed a yard short can carry greater expected value than reaching the marker. The full derivation and data are in the paper and accompanying notebook.

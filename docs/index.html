An interactive proof · Sports analytics

The line is not
the finish line.

For a hundred years, football has treated the first-down marker as a place to reach. The data says otherwise: on first down, a ball-carrier who can only just stretch to the marker is often better off going down a yard short. Scroll, drag, and run the numbers yourself.

See why ↓
Act 1 — The hook

Where would you go down?

Here's a first-down run. Drag the ball-carrier to choose where he ends the play. Watch the expected points for the drive — the true scoreboard — as you cross, or stop short of, the marker.

Drag the ● along the field. Then ask: did crossing the line actually help?

Act 2 — The tell

The notch in the data

This is what really happens on first-and-ten runs. Look at the bin sitting right on the marker. Ball-carriers treat the line as a finish line — the instant they cross it, they stop trying. The distribution collapses exactly where the first down is won.

The highlighted bar is the yard the whole league stops fighting for.

Act 3 — The currency

Yards aren't the point. Points are.

Everything reduces to one exchange rate: where you are on the field, in expected points. Roughly zero at your own 20, about four near your opponent's. So ~15 yards buys a point — and one whole possession is worth about 40 yards. Drag the ball to feel the curve.

A lost possession costs ≈ −2 points. Hold that number; Act 4 needs it.

Act 4 — The showdown

2nd-and-1 beats 1st-and-10

Here's the heresy, made testable. Compare two states from the same spot: take the first down now (1st-and-10), or stop a yard short (2nd-and-1) and rush it out. Simulate thousands of drives and watch which state scores more. Tune the assumptions — the verdict holds.

Press Run 10,000 drives. Then move the sliders and re-run.

Act 5 — A bonus heresy

3rd-and-1: should you pass?

While we're upending things: on 3rd-and-1 the run is "safe," but the pass — though it converts a little less often — gains far more when it lands, and the defense never expects it. Net it out in expected points.

Toggle the call and compare the expected-points payoff.

So fix first down.

The marker is an accounting line, not a goal. Coached well, a yard short can be worth more than a yard past — because a near-certain conversion on the next play lands you further downfield with a fresh set of downs. The math has been sitting in plain sight.
